From the above we can see as p increases to 1, the average shortest path length falls very rapidly, quickly approaching its limiting value. The rapid drop in d(p) signals the onset of the small-world phenomenon. As p inceases, so does the randomness of the network increases. For p = 1 all links have been rewired, so the network turns into a random network.

Power-laws and the friendship paradox¶

Next step is to explore the Friendship paradox. This paradox states that almost everyone have fewer friends than their friends have, on average. This sounds crazy, but is actually an almost trivial consequence of living in a social network with a power-law degree distribution. The explanation is that almost everyone is friends with a hub, that drives up the average degree of the friends. Let's explore that in the 5000 node BA network we've just generated. Do the following:

  • Pick a node i at random (e.g. use random.choice). Find its degree.
  • Find i's neighbors. And calculate their average degree.
  • Compare the two numbers to check if it's true that i's friends (on average) have more friends than i.
  • Do this 1000 times. How many out of those 1000 times is the friendship paradox true?

We can see that the degree distribution p_k of this ER network is approximately a Poisson degree distribution. However, in the BA network without preferential attachment, the degree distribution p_k is not Poisson, which decreases much more slowly than the random network.

This means that there are more high degree nodes and low degree nodes in BA network graph than in ER network. And some of them can be really big or small.

The significant deviation between the two networks indicates that the ER network model underestimates the size and the frequency of the high degree nodes, as well as the number of low degree nodes. Instead the ER network model predicts a larger number of nodes in the vicinity of ‹k› than seen in real networks.

Degree distributions continued¶

With the network G in place, we can now start to analyze it. First of all we note that we have used a directed graph. We do this since a philosopher can link to another philosopher, without getting a link back. This is often the case with the old philosophers, that have a lot of other philosophers linking to them. Having a high degree means that you are a famous and well known philosopher. So if we have used a undirected graph in stead, all the lesser known philosophers would have high degrees, as often link to more philosophers. Thus a directed graph seems like the proper choice here.
